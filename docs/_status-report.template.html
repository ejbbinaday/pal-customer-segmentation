01 The short version

The goal is to sort PAL's passengers into ten segments that actually mean something commercially — Corporate, Mabuhay Loyalist, OFW/Migrant, Balikbayan/VFR, Family, Pilgrimage, Premium Bleisure, Budget/Adventure, Last-Minute and Digital Nomad. The catch is that we have to do it blind: no names, no loyalty IDs, nothing that links a booking back to a person we already know. That isn't a corner we cut — it's a recognised approach in the industry, the “anonymous segmentation” lens, where you read a trip's purpose and its value from what the booking itself tells you.

Once we swapped the early synthetic prototype for PAL's real 38-million-coupon extract, the data pushed us into one big change of plan — and it was the evidence that made the call, not a preference of ours:

The headline decision

PAL's customers don't fall into neat, separate clusters — they form a continuum. So the workhorse is a transparent, rule-based purpose×value classifier, and machine-learning clustering (Latent Class Analysis) plays a supporting role: it refines and validates rather than labels. Density-based clustering (HDBSCAN) was dropped for the real data.

Where we are today: every one of the 22.9M bookings has a segment label, and — reassuringly — average revenue climbs steadily from one segment to the next, which is exactly what we'd hope to see if the segments are real. The one thing still missing is a ground-truth check, and for that we need a small labelled sample from PAL.

02 What PAL actually sent us

The extract arrives at coupon level — one row per flight leg, 38.1M of them, covering departures from May 2024 to May 2027, and stripped of anything that identifies a person. PAL's authoritative V1 data dictionary put right two things we'd assumed from the older sample, and each one nudged the design:

  • UniqueID turns out to be a customer key, not a booking reference — we confirmed it by watching the same IDs reappear over spans of up to ~3.2 years. That's a gift: it means we can actually track repeat behaviour, tenure and loyalty for the travellers who come back.
  • Every booking class maps onto an official farebrand value ladder, from Business Flex right down to Economy Supersaver — a clean, ordered measure of value that let us retire the fare tiers we'd been guessing at.

A trap that would have burned us

Booking classes F and G quietly swap meaning on 1 April 2026. Before that date G means an award redemption; after it, F means award and G means a group fare. Take the raw numbers at face value and you'd see a “surge in loyalty” that never happened. Applying the date-aware rule instead, we correctly pick up ~9,150 award coupons across the full span — versus roughly 1,000 the naïve reading would catch.

A few other quirks shape the work. Age is missing on 57% of rows, but not randomly — it's only captured on international operations, so we treat “do we even know the age?” as a signal in itself and never let it drive the segments. The carrier code is the same value on every row, so it's dropped. And revenue has a long tail with refunds and zeros mixed in, so we repair those rather than mistake a refund for a bargain fare.

Choosing the unit of analysis

A single coupon is too fine-grained to carry a trip's purpose, and a whole customer is too coarse. The sweet spot is a booking — one purchase decision — which we rebuild as (UniqueID, DateOfIssuance). The data backs this up: 43% of bookings hold a full round-trip, outbound and return together. The obvious alternative, keying on individual trips, averaged just 1.13 legs each and sliced every return flight off into a phantom one-way. So we build features at booking level first, then roll them up to the customer.

A caveat we keep in plain sight

Only about a quarter of customers ever book more than once. For them, tenure, frequency and lifetime value are genuinely informative. For the other three-quarters, the customer simply is their single booking — so loyalty rests on award flags and that repeat-booking minority, not on rich histories we don't have. We'd rather say so up front than pretend otherwise.

03 Getting to know the data (EDA)

Before modelling anything, we spent time simply looking at the data (all 22.9M bookings, aggregated in DuckDB). Two things stood out and steered everything after. First, PAL's network is overwhelmingly domestic, and most of that travel is cheap economy — which means value on its own can't tell the segments apart. What does separate them is where people are flying: domestic versus international. Second, booking timing splits into two crowds — a burst of last-minute travellers and a long tail of people who book weeks or months ahead.

04 How the pipeline works

38 million rows is too much to hold in memory all at once, so the heavy lifting happens in DuckDB, which streams the raw files and boils them down before anything touches pandas. The intermediate results are saved as fast Parquet files, and only the aggregated, model-ready table gets handed to pandas and scikit-learn. Everything below is a single script in src/, run in order.

  1. P
    Typed Parquet build
    Raw gzip → typed, partitioned Parquet. Repairs timestamps, money strings and encoding.
    build_parquet.py
  2. C
    Clean & flag — coupon grain

    clean_real.py · build_airport_ref.py
  3. E
    Exploratory analysis
    Aggregate EDA on value, route, timing and customer behaviour (§3). Key finding: value alone is non-discriminative, so the route split becomes essential.
    eda_real.py
  4. F
    Feature engineering — coupon → booking → customer
    Four feature families (value, loyalty, timing, product/route) rebuilt for the real schema, plus an airport→region join and a customer rollup.
    features_real.py
  5. 1°
    Rule-based purpose×value proxy — PRIMARY deliverable
    A priority waterfall assigns each booking to one of the 10 segments (or an explicit Unassigned bucket). This is the labeller.
    the 10 named segments
  6. L

Why the rules lead and clustering follows

This was the big question, and we didn't want to answer it on instinct — so we tested it. We ran two different clustering methods — Latent Class Analysis and k-prototypes — on a 60,000-booking sample across 11 features, and asked them plainly: is there natural structure here? The answer came back no, twice over. The model-fit score keeps improving as you add clusters, with no natural stopping point, and whatever clusters do emerge only loosely match the business segments (agreement never tops ARI 0.34) — and when they split, they split along the exact same lines our rules were already drawing.

Flattening the sample onto two dimensions makes the point visible at a glance. There are no clean gaps between the clusters, and colouring the same points by our rule segments shows them bleeding into one another rather than sitting in tidy islands — the signature of a continuum. In short, clustering was just re-deriving our own rules, only less cleanly. So we let the transparent, auditable rules do the labelling, and put clustering to work where it genuinely helps: teasing out sub-types within the big segments. HDBSCAN, which hunts for dense pockets, was retired here — travel data this categorical simply doesn't come in dense pockets.

05 What we built it with

No black boxes and no proprietary analytics platform — the whole pipeline is open-source Python. We only bring in a heavy-data engine where the 38 million rows genuinely need one, and keep the modelling on well-worn, auditable libraries so anyone can reproduce the work or pick it up later. 06 What we're seeing so far

Every one of the 22.9M bookings now carries a segment label. The check we cared about most: does value line up the way it should? It does — average spend rises steadily right up the ladder, from $74 a booking for Budget/Adventure to $1,504 for Premium Bleisure. If the segments were arbitrary, you wouldn't see that; the fact that you do is a good early sign we're capturing something real.

Digging inside the big segments

This is where clustering earns its keep. Some segments are large enough that “Budget/Adventure” alone doesn't tell marketing much, so LCA breaks the biggest ones into a handful of practical sub-types (we cap the number at what's actually useful, since — as everywhere in this data — there's no natural break point). Budget/Adventure, for instance, falls into four: the bargain one-way supersaver ($23), a one-way saver booked a couple of weeks out ($69), the biggest group by far — a round-trip saver booked in advance ($87, 38% of the segment) — and a connecting one-way ($86). OFW and Balikbayan each break into four along similar lines of timing, connections and value; Last-Minute into three. The headline ten stay exactly as they are — these sub-types just add colour underneath.

07 Being straight about what the data can and can't do

The ten segments come from the business, not the data — and anonymous data supports some of them far better than others. We'd much rather flag that now than let it surface later. Realistically, five or six segments come through with real confidence; the rest are either thin, overlap heavily with a neighbour, or lean on route lookups we've had to build by hand. 08 What's left, and what we need from PAL

The pipeline is built and has run end to end through feature engineering, and the method is settled. What's left is mostly closing the validation gap and talking through a few taxonomy questions with PAL — several of these need input from your side, not just more analysis from ours.

PAL A labelled sample

Right now we're grading our own homework — validation compares against the same rules that made the labels. A small set of examples labelled by your experts would break that circle, and it's the single most valuable thing we could get.

Discuss The Unassigned 9.6%

Mostly Philippines-issued outbound international economy — a genuine travel pattern that none of the ten segments quite fits. We'd like to decide together where it belongs.

Enrich Mabuhay Loyalist

Very thin today (0.03%) because we can only see award redemptions. We can widen it using repeat-booking and premium-cabin behaviour once we work at customer level.

Note Digital Nomad has no anchor

There's simply no reliable way to spot one in anonymous data as the segment is defined today. Worth a conversation — either we redefine it or accept it won't appear.

One framing worth stating plainly: we're treating the ten-segment list as a well-informed hypothesis to test against PAL's own knowledge, not a fixed target to hit. How many segments cleanly emerge, and exactly how each is defined, is a finding that comes out of the work — not a promise we made going in.
